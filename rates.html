DTE Residential Rate Plans

In March 2023 DTE moved every residential customer onto D1.11 Time-of-Day (3pm–7pm) by default. That single change made battery arbitrage and solar self-consumption far more valuable. This page documents every plan a Southeast Michigan residential customer can choose today, the period rates for each, and the common charges and net-metering rules that apply on top.

All rates effective early 2026, sourced from current DTE rate cards. Stated peak/off-peak prices already include the 9.726¢/kWh distribution charge. PSCR and other riders are excluded.

D1.11 — Time of Day 3pm–7pm Default plan

Summary. The default plan since March 2023. A short 4-hour peak window (3pm–7pm weekdays) — the shortest of any DTE TOU plan. Off-peak is the same rate year-round (18.44¢/kWh); the only thing that changes between summer and winter is the peak rate. Easy to live with, but the off-peak rate is relatively expensive, which limits arbitrage upside.

D1.2 — Time of Day 11am–7pm

Summary. An older, wider TOU plan with an 8-hour peak window but a noticeably cheaper off-peak rate (15.18¢ summer / 14.97¢ winter). Worth considering if your loads are easy to push outside 11am–7pm — for example, a household that already shifts laundry, dishwasher, and EV charging to overnight. The cheap off-peak makes this attractive for solar self-consumption: the value of every solar kWh that offsets a peak-hour load is large (25.93¢ summer).

D1.13 — Overnight Savers Best for battery

Summary. The most aggressive three-tier plan DTE offers. Super off-peak from 1am–7am is just 11.74¢/kWh — the cheapest rate available on any DTE plan. Summer peak is 35.56¢/kWh, producing a 23.82¢/kWh arbitrage spread. Customers with a battery can charge overnight at super off-peak and discharge during peak; customers with an EV gain the same overnight benefit without any battery investment at all.

Why D1.13 is the right plan for battery owners

The economics of a home battery are driven almost entirely by the spread between the cheapest hour you can charge and the most expensive hour you can avoid. On D1.13 that spread is 23.82¢/kWh in summer (35.56¢ peak − 11.74¢ super off-peak) — more than 4× the spread on the default D1.11 plan. A 19 kWh usable battery cycled once per day yields roughly $4.50/day in summer arbitrage value before any solar is added. Every Stage 1 build on this site assumes a switch to D1.13.

D1.1 — Cool Currents (central AC on a separate meter)

Summary. DTE installs a second meter dedicated to your central air conditioner. Energy used by the AC is billed on Cool Currents — a flat, capacity-based rate that works out to roughly 17¢/kWh in summer and 15¢/kWh in winter. In exchange, DTE may briefly cycle your AC compressor during high-demand events. Cool Currents is additive to your main TOU plan — your house keeps its D1.11 / D1.2 / D1.13 rate; only the AC moves.

Distributed Generation (Net-Metering Replacement)

Michigan replaced traditional net metering with an inflow / outflow Distributed Generation (DG) program. The grid measures the kWh you import and the kWh you export separately, and each direction is priced differently. Exports do not offset imports 1-for-1.

  • Inflow and outflow are measured separately — you pay full retail for imports and receive a much lower export credit for outflows.
  • Export rate (off-peak): ~7.75¢/kWh — well below the off-peak retail rate on any plan.
  • Export rate (summer peak): ~14¢/kWh — still less than half of D1.13's 35.56¢ summer peak retail.
  • 20 kW Level 1 interconnection — most residential systems qualify for the simplest interconnection path.
  • $50 one-time interconnection fee.
  • 110% sizing cap — your DG nameplate cannot exceed 110% of your previous 12-month usage.
  • 6% statewide DG cap — DTE territory has not yet hit this cap but it is worth watching.

Implication: maximize self-consumption. Don't oversize solar — pair with battery.

Because export credits are roughly half the retail off-peak rate (and a quarter of the peak rate), a solar kWh consumed in your home is worth roughly 2× to 4× more than a solar kWh exported to the grid. The right system design is therefore the smallest array that covers your daytime load, paired with a battery that stores excess for evening peak — not the largest array that fits on your roof.

No 30% Federal Tax Credit (effective Jan 1, 2026)

The 30% federal residential solar tax credit (Section 25D of the Internal Revenue Code) was eliminated for customer-owned residential solar and battery systems effective January 1, 2026 by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025. Every payback calculation on this site assumes a federal credit of 0%. Third-party owned systems (leases and PPAs) may still qualify under the Section 48E commercial credit if construction begins before July 2026, but homeowners do not directly benefit.
