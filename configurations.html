Example System Configurations

Five buildable, real-world configurations for DTE residential customers in Southeast Michigan — ranging from a $1,450 comfort upgrade (Cool Currents + mini-split) to a ~$15,899 full electrification build (home + EV + heat pump). Every price assumes zero federal tax credit and DIY-friendly equipment available from US distributors.

Configuration A — Battery-Only Arbitrage (No Solar)

Best for: Renters with landlord permission, homes with shaded or non-viable roofs, households waiting on a roof replacement before adding solar.

ComponentModelPrice
InverterEG4 12kPV Hybrid 48V$4,049
Battery4× EG4 LL-S 48V 100Ah (19.2 kWh)$5,720
Total$9,769

How it works

Switch to D1.13 Overnight Savers. Charge the battery at 11.74¢/kWh during the 1am–7am super off-peak window. Discharge during peak (35.56¢ summer / 19.06¢ winter) and off-peak (25.46¢ summer / 15.55¢ winter) hours. No solar interconnection, no roof work, no permitting beyond the inverter — a self-contained arbitrage box.

Annual savings: $450–550 battery-only, $700+ with EV.
Payback: ~14–18 years battery-only, ~10–12 years with EV.

How it works

On D1.13 Overnight Savers. Solar charges the battery during the day and offsets daytime household load directly. The battery discharges during the 3pm–7pm peak. Whatever the battery cannot cover by evening is topped off overnight from the 11.74¢ super off-peak grid. The 3.2 kW array is intentionally right-sized for self-consumption — not exports — given DTE's low outflow rate.

Annual savings: $800–900.
Payback: ~13–14 years. 25-year savings: ~$15,000+.

How it works

Built for the 50–70 kWh/day a fully electrified household consumes (home + EV + heat pump in winter). Larger array sized for winter heat-pump production; oversized battery absorbs solar midday and discharges against the long winter evening peak. EV charges overnight at super off-peak.

Annual savings: $1,500–2,000.
Payback: ~8–10 years. 25-year savings: ~$30,000+.

How it works

Enroll the central AC in Cool Currents — it moves to a flat ~17¢/kWh summer rate on a separate meter. The mini-split handles problem rooms and provides backup when DTE briefly cycles the central unit during high-demand events. The ERV provides MERV 13-filtered fresh air at just ~8W draw, improving comfort and indoor air quality.

Annual savings: Varies — primarily comfort + AC cost shift.
Payback: Comfort benefit dominates ROI.

Configuration E — RECOMMENDED — Victron + Docan Panda (Staged)

Best for: DIY homeowners staging investment over several years, maximizing battery arbitrage today, and adding solar when the roof is ready.

This is the build this site recommends for most DTE customers who can do their own installation. It starts as a battery-only arbitrage system, becomes a battery+solar system at Stage 2 without throwing anything away, and can scale to EV + heat pump loads at Stage 3.

Add capacity only as new electrified loads arrive. Each addition slots into the same Cerbo GX-managed Victron system. There is no forklift upgrade.

Why Victron for staged builds

  • Each stage is independently useful — nothing thrown away when the next stage is added.
  • Dynamic ESS handles rate optimization automatically — no manual scheduling.
  • Modular: add inverters, MPPTs, and batteries as budget allows.
  • 48V system is safe for DIY maintenance and expansion.
  • Works with virtually any 48V battery — you are not locked to one brand or generation.
  • Lower upfront cost than an EG4 all-in-one when solar is not needed yet.

Why Docan Panda

  • 32 kWh for ~$2,755 = ~$86/kWh — vs ~$298/kWh for an equivalent EG4 LL-S stack.
  • CALB 314Ah LiFePO4 cells with an 8,000-cycle life.
  • 200A continuous discharge and a 10-year warranty.
  • Ships from a Houston, TX warehouse — pickup option saves the $560 freight charge.
  • 51.2V nominal — compatible with virtually any 48V inverter (Victron, EG4, Sol-Ark, Schneider).

Caveat: Docan Panda is a Chinese direct-import brand

Warranty support is less established than name-brand alternatives like EG4 or LG. Buyers comfortable with DIY troubleshooting and direct-from-manufacturer warranty workflows accept this tradeoff for the ~3.5× price advantage. Risk-averse buyers should prefer EG4 LL-S or an LG residential battery and accept the much higher per-kWh cost.

Real-World Reference Case — Dearborn, MI

The recommended configurations on this site are calibrated against a real Southeast Michigan home in Dearborn, MI. The home's measured load profile drives the array sizing and battery sizing assumptions used throughout the calculator and configurations above.

Measured Load Profile

  • Daily main-meter usage: 18.86 kWh
  • Overnight baseline: 64 W
  • Peak 15-minute demand: 2.185 kW
  • Top loads: furnace blower (55%), kitchen (17%), living room (15%)

Current Setup

  • Current rate plan: D1.11 (3pm–7pm default)
  • Cool Currents: enrolled (central AC on separate meter)
  • Solar: none yet
  • Battery: none yet
