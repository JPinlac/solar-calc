DTE Rate Projections & Payback Analysis

DTE's residential rates have climbed faster than general inflation for a decade, and the pace is accelerating. For long-term planning we use a 5.5% annual growth scenario — aggressive, but well-justified by the last four years of rate-case activity, data center load growth, coal retirements, and the Trenton Channel battery buildout. All payback math on this page assumes that scenario and zero federal tax credit.

Historical Rate Trajectory

DTE rate increases added $774 million in costs between 2015–2019. A $368 million increase followed in 2023, then $217 million in January 2025, with another $456 million requested in April 2025. Rate case filings have accelerated from every 24 months a decade ago to every 12 months now.

Compound Annual Growth Rates

  • 10-year CAGR (2015–2025): 3.4%
  • Recent 4-year CAGR (2021–2025): ~3.8%
  • Peak recent years: 4–5%+ (2021, 2023, 2025)

Why 5.5% Is the Right Planning Assumption

Historical CAGR sits in the 3–4% range, but every forward-looking pressure points up, not down. A 5.5% planning assumption is conservative against the worst case and still leaves margin for surprises.

    Battery Arbitrage Payback Analysis

    Methodology

    • Without system: D1.11 (default plan, 3–7pm peak)
    • With system: D1.13 Overnight Savers + battery arbitrage
    • Battery charges 1am–7am super off-peak (11.74¢ in 2026, +5.5%/yr)
    • 90% round-trip efficiency on battery discharge
    • House baseline: 19 kWh/day; ~4.75 kWh consumed directly during the super off-peak window
    • All rates inflate 5.5%/yr

    Scenario C: House + 2 EVs (second EV added Year 3)

    Second EV averages 10 kWh/day. Both EVs charge during super off-peak (combined 22 kWh/day). Note: 22 + 19 = 41 kWh/day. Six-hour super off-peak window → ~6.8 kW average charge rate, achievable with two Level 2 chargers or one staggered 40A charger — verify panel capacity. Battery handles house only; both EVs charge straight from the grid during super off-peak.
