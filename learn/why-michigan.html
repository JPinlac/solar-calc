Why This Works in Michigan — Even Without the Tax Credit

The 30% federal residential solar tax credit (Section 25D) was eliminated on January 1, 2026. Every calculation on this site assumes zero federal incentive. The good news: the economics for DTE customers in Southeast Michigan still work — they just stand on rate arbitrage, rising utility rates, and self-consumption rather than a subsidy.

The 30% Federal Tax Credit Is GONE as of 2026

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025, eliminated the Section 25D residential clean energy credit for customer-owned systems placed in service on or after January 1, 2026. Third-party owned systems (leases / PPAs) may still qualify under Section 48E commercial credits, but homeowners no longer benefit directly. System economics must now stand on their own — and as the rest of this page explains, they do.

For two decades, the federal Investment Tax Credit was the single biggest line in any residential solar quote. Its removal collapsed a huge marketing engine overnight: lease companies, third-party finance shops, and door-to-door sales operations were built around being able to say "30% off." Without it, mainstream residential solar in the US looks very different in 2026.

But the question is not "is solar as attractive as it was in 2024?" — it isn't. The question is "does it still pay off in Michigan today, paying full price out of pocket?" The answer, for DTE customers who right-size the system and pair it with the right rate plan, is yes. Here is why.

The Nine Reasons It Still Pencils Out

1. Michigan has Germany's solar resource

Southeast Michigan sits at roughly 42° N latitude — almost identical to Munich. Germany is the world leader in residential solar PV deployment by capita, generating more than 10% of its national electricity from rooftop solar. If solar works at scale in Bavaria, it works in Dearborn. Michigan actually has slightly better summer sun than central Germany; the climates differ on heating loads, not on photovoltaics.

2. DTE rates climb 3–5% per year

DTE's residential rates have risen from roughly 14.5¢/kWh blended in 2015 to over 20¢/kWh in 2026 — a steady compounding increase. With Michigan's data-center build-out, coal plant retirements, grid hardening, and battery storage capital recovery all hitting at once, the trajectory is more likely to steepen than flatten. Every year you wait, the cost of the electricity you're displacing goes up.

3. Battery prices are in free fall

A Docan Panda 32 kWh LiFePO4 pack lands at roughly $2,755 — about $86 per kWh before shipping. Five years ago, comparable storage cost $400–600/kWh. At today's prices, battery arbitrage on DTE's Overnight Savers plan can pay for the battery alone (no solar required) in 8–12 years on a typical household.

4. 48V systems are safe for DIY

48-volt DC nominal (51.2V LiFePO4) is well below the 60V threshold typically considered shock-hazardous to a healthy adult. Touching the terminals is unpleasant, not lethal. That changes the math entirely for the homeowner who wants to assemble and maintain the battery stack themselves and only sub out the licensed-electrician portion of the work. High-voltage (400V) systems are a different story and should be professionally installed.

5. Victron + Dynamic ESS automates arbitrage

Victron's Cerbo GX paired with the MultiPlus-II inverter family runs Dynamic ESS — a built-in optimizer that reads DTE-style time-of-use schedules and decides when to charge from grid, when to charge from solar, and when to discharge to the home. You do not need to write any custom logic; the ecosystem ships with the optimization. It is the closest thing to set-and- forget battery arbitrage available to a residential customer.

6. EG4 is built for homeowner installation

EG4 Electronics has explicitly designed its product line — the 12kPV, 18kPV, and LL-S battery modules — around the homeowner / electrician installer pair. Documentation is thorough, firmware updates are public, and Signature Solar's YouTube and community forum make troubleshooting accessible. If Victron's modular philosophy is too parts-bin for your taste, EG4 is the all-in-one alternative.

7. DTE interconnection is cheap

For systems under 20 kW, DTE charges a flat $50 interconnection fee. That is it — no per-kW surcharge, no engineering review fee, no usurious meter-swap charge. Compared to other US utilities that nickel-and-dime residential interconnections, DTE's process is almost embarrassingly affordable.

8. Zero-export bypasses DG complexity

If you size your system around self-consumption and configure the inverter to never export to the grid, you avoid DTE's distributed-generation program entirely — no inflow/outflow metering, no special tariff, no application beyond the basic interconnection. This is the path of least resistance for a battery-first build and aligns naturally with the fact that DTE's export rates are far below retail anyway.

9. The plan switch alone pays

Before you spend a dollar on equipment, switching from D1.11 to D1.13 Overnight Savers can save the right household money if you can shift even a modest fraction of usage (EV charging, dishwasher, laundry, water heater) into the 1am–7am super off-peak window at 11.74¢/kWh. That is a free first step that builds the case for adding battery later.

What Replaces the Tax Credit, Mathematically

The ITC used to provide a one-time 30% reduction in upfront cost. What is replacing it, in 2026, is a combination of three slower-acting forces that together exceed the credit over a 10-year horizon:

  • Battery cost compression — at $86/kWh, a 32 kWh pack costs less today than the ITC discount on a 2022-era 20 kWh pack would have been.
  • TOU rate spread widening — the gap between super off-peak (11.74¢) and peak (35.56¢) is 23.82¢/kWh. That is the arbitrage opportunity, and it grows in absolute terms every rate case.
  • Self-consumption value — every kWh you generate and use yourself is worth full retail (~20¢) avoided, not the ~8¢ DTE pays you to export it.

Put together: a system built today at full price is paying back against a higher avoided rate than the same system would have been paying against in 2022, even before the credit. The math is tighter than it used to be, but it still works — provided you choose the right architecture.

The Quiet Advantage of Paying Full Price

Without the ITC, the door-to-door sales model collapses. The systems being installed in 2026 are the ones whose owners did the math themselves. That means less pressure to oversize, less pressure to finance at usurious rates, and a much higher proportion of right-sized DIY and owner-builder systems — the configurations that actually pay back fastest.
