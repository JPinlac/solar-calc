Understanding Your DTE Bill

Before you can decide whether solar, battery, or a rate-plan switch will save you money, you have to know what you are actually being charged for. DTE's residential bill is more layered than most. This page walks through every line you'll see, what it means, and how to read your hourly usage data so you can size a system correctly.

Capacity vs Non-Capacity Charges

Almost every DTE rate plan splits energy charges into two regulatory buckets: capacity and non-capacity. The split is invisible if you only ever look at the bottom line, but it matters because the two pieces respond differently in rate cases and the two pieces are quoted separately on some rate sheets.

  • Capacity recovers DTE's investment in generation capacity — the cost of owning power plants, transmission interconnects, and reserve margin. It is roughly the fixed cost of being able to deliver a kilowatt at peak.
  • Non-capacity recovers the variable costs of generating that kilowatt-hour — fuel, plant operations and maintenance, ancillary services.

On most plans the capacity component is much higher in the four summer months (June–September) than the rest of the year, which is why summer rates are visibly higher. The non-capacity piece is more stable. When you see "energy charge" or "supply charge" on the bill, that's capacity + non-capacity added back together.

The Distribution Charge: 9.726¢/kWh, Year-Round

Separately from the energy / supply charges, DTE applies a distribution charge of 9.726 cents per kWh to every single kilowatt-hour you consume. This is the cost of the wires, poles, transformers, and local substations between you and the generation. It does not vary by time of day, by season, or by rate plan.

The distribution charge is the largest single line item on most residential bills — bigger than the capacity energy charge, bigger than the non-capacity charge. When people quote a single "effective rate" for DTE — around 20¢/kWh blended — roughly half of that number is distribution. This matters for two reasons:

  • Self-generated and self-consumed kWh avoid the distribution charge. Solar that powers your fridge directly is saving you 20¢, not 10¢.
  • Exported kWh do not earn the distribution component back — DTE's export credit is roughly the energy supply piece only, which is why the export rate looks so low.

PSCR — Power Supply Cost Recovery

The published TOU rate cards do not include the Power Supply Cost Recovery rider. PSCR is a monthly true-up that lets DTE pass through the difference between projected fuel and energy market costs and actual costs. It appears as a separate line on your bill and can swing positive or negative month-to-month, though in practice it has been positive most months for the last several years.

A typical residential PSCR adjustment is a few tenths of a cent per kWh, sometimes more during periods of high natural gas prices. It applies on top of the stated TOU rates. Because it varies, payback modeling on this site uses the published rate-card rates without PSCR and notes the rider separately.

Inflow vs Outflow Under the DG Program

DTE no longer offers true net metering for new customers. The replacement is the Distributed Generation program, which measures grid imports and exports separately and instantaneously — not netted over a billing period. This is the single most important thing to understand if you are sizing a system.

  • Inflow (grid → home) is billed at the full TOU rate for whatever period you are in. Same as a non-solar customer.
  • Outflow (home → grid) earns a credit at the DG export rate — currently roughly 7.75¢ off-peak and ~14¢ summer peak. These are far below retail rates.

Critically, because measurement is instantaneous, a kilowatt-hour exported at 2pm cannot offset a kilowatt-hour imported at 8pm. The export earns its export rate; the import is billed separately at the retail rate. There is no monthly bank or roll-over.

Implication for system design

Because exports pay roughly a third of what imports cost, the dollar value of every kWh of solar depends entirely on whether you consume it yourself or send it back. A right-sized system with battery storage that shifts midday solar into evening peak captures full retail value on most of its production. An oversized panel-only system gives away a large fraction of its output at the low export rate.

The Service Charge and Other Fixed Items

DTE charges a fixed $8.50/month service charge regardless of usage. This is the cost of having the connection at all — meter reading, billing, customer service overhead. Solar does not avoid this charge: even a customer who exports more than they import will still owe $8.50/month. There is also a small RIA (Renewable Integration Assessment) credit of $-8.50 that effectively offsets the service charge for many customers; check your bill to confirm whether it applies.

For battery-only / zero-export builds, the service charge is essentially a floor you cannot beat. Plan accordingly: the smallest possible DTE bill for a still-connected home is around $9–10 plus a tiny amount of distribution and PSCR even if you import almost nothing.

How to Download Your Hourly Usage Data

DTE provides every residential customer with hourly interval data through the Energy Bridge / Green Button download feature in the online account portal. Pull this before you size any system — sizing on monthly totals alone is a recipe for an oversized array or undersized battery.

  1. Log in to your DTE account at dteenergy.com.
  2. Navigate to Energy Usage → Download My Data (sometimes labeled "Green Button Download").
  3. Select a CSV format and a date range — at least 12 months if you have it.
  4. The file will contain one row per hour with a start timestamp and kWh consumed during that hour.

With a year of hourly data you can answer the questions that actually drive sizing: what is my overnight baseline draw, when does my real peak hit (is it 3–7pm or actually 5–9pm in your household?), how much usage already falls in the super off-peak window before any behavioral change, and what fraction of summer usage is the central AC. The reference household on this site runs an 18.86 kWh/day average with a 64W overnight baseline — yours will be different, and the only way to know is to look.
