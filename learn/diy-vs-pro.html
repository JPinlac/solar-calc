DIY vs Professional Install

The cheapest residential solar + battery installs in 2026 are almost always hybrid — homeowner-built where the law and good sense allow it, and licensed-electrician-finished where they don't. This page explains what Michigan actually requires, what tasks the homeowner can safely do, what the DTE interconnection process looks like end to end, and where the real risks live.

Michigan Licensing Requirements

Michigan requires that any electrical work that connects to the home's main service panel or to the utility grid be performed or directly supervised by a licensed electrical contractor. Practically, that means the final grid-tie, the panel breaker installation, and the inspection sign-off must come from someone with a Michigan electrical license. This is not optional and not negotiable with your AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction — usually the city building department).

What Michigan does not require a license for varies by municipality, but generally includes assembling battery racks, mounting solar panel racking on a roof, running DC conduit between the array and the inverter location, and assembling the battery bank (busbars, fuses, BMS wiring). The law's line is roughly "before the inverter on the DC side, owner-builder is allowed; from the inverter through the panel to the meter, licensed only."

Even within the DIY-allowed portion, the homeowner-builder exemption typically only applies to single-family owner-occupied homes. If you own a rental property, the exemption does not apply and all the work must be done under license.

Tasks Homeowners Can Reasonably Do

A homeowner with reasonable mechanical and electrical aptitude — the kind of person who has wired up a 240V dryer outlet or installed a ceiling fan — can do a substantial fraction of a solar + battery install themselves:

  • Site selection and design — roof orientation, shading analysis, array layout, system sizing. This is the part that most affects payback and is often done worse by sales-driven installers than by a careful homeowner with a year of hourly data.
  • Panel mounting and racking — flashing, rail attachment, panel clamping. Anyone who can install a ridge vent can install solar rails. The risk here is fall protection and water sealing, not electrical.
  • Battery rack assembly — racking the modules, torquing the busbar bolts, terminating the BMS and balance leads. With 48V LiFePO4, this is well within homeowner reach.
  • DC conduit and wire runs — from array combiner to MPPT, from MPPT to inverter, from battery to inverter. Many municipalities allow this under the homeowner exemption; check yours.
  • Inverter and Cerbo GX wall-mount — physical mounting and DC-side wiring. AC-side wiring waits for the electrician.
  • Commissioning configuration — VictronConnect setup, Cerbo network setup, Dynamic ESS schedule, EG4 monitor setup. Software-side work that no contractor will do for you anyway.

The Subcontracting Model

The model most owner-builders use looks like this: the homeowner designs the system, sources all the equipment, mounts the panels, builds the battery, runs the DC, and stages the inverter ready to be wired. Then a licensed electrician is hired for a half-day or full-day visit to do the AC-side work — pull the main, install the backfeed breaker (or critical-load subpanel), terminate the inverter AC output, and sign off on the work.

In Southeast Michigan, going rates for a half-day electrician visit on a pre-staged solar/battery install run roughly $500–1,200 depending on the complexity of the panel work. This is a small fraction of what a full turnkey install would cost, and it preserves the homeowner's full control over equipment choice and system architecture. Some electricians specialize in this hybrid model and will help you plan the rough-in so they can do the finish work efficiently.

Important: the electrician must be willing to put their license on a system that they did not install soup-to-nuts. Not all of them will. Have this conversation up front, ideally before you buy equipment, so the electrician can review your inverter and battery choices and confirm they are comfortable signing off on the install.

NABCEP Certification (For the Truly Committed)

NABCEP (the North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners) offers the industry-standard solar and battery certifications: PV Associate, PV Installation Professional, and PV Technical Sales. These are not Michigan licenses — they are voluntary industry credentials — but they are widely recognized and a path for someone who wants to move from DIY-on-my-own-house to doing it professionally.

For most homeowners doing one installation, NABCEP is overkill. But if you find yourself helping neighbors, considering a career change, or you simply want the structured training, the PV Associate exam is a reasonable entry point. The associate-level credential combined with a Michigan electrical license is a complete professional toolkit.

The DTE Interconnection Process, Step by Step

Whether your system is DIY, hybrid, or fully professional, if it will export to the grid (or even if it is a parallel-tied non-export system) you go through DTE's interconnection process. For residential systems under 20 kW the process is relatively streamlined:

  1. Application — submit DTE's interconnection application with system schematics, equipment cut sheets, and the $50 fee. Most of the form-filling happens here.
  2. DTE approval — DTE reviews and issues a conditional approval to construct. Typical turnaround is 2–6 weeks. Do not start work expecting to be done before this approval; some homeowners pre-stage but do not energize.
  3. Install — perform the physical install, hybrid or otherwise. Document everything with photographs as you go — your AHJ inspector will appreciate it and so will DTE if there are questions later.
  4. AHJ inspection — your city or township building department inspects the finished work. This is the licensed electrician's sign-off step. Once passed, the AHJ issues an inspection certificate.
  5. DTE meter swap / turn-on — submit the AHJ certificate to DTE. They schedule a meter swap (if needed for bidirectional metering) and authorize commercial operation. Only after this step can you legally energize the inverter and export to the grid.

For pure zero-export systems (battery-only, no grid export ever), some homeowners skip the DG portion entirely and just file standard electrical permits. This is much simpler administratively and is one reason the battery-first / zero-export approach is so popular for DIY builds.

Permitting Varies by Municipality

Michigan does not have a unified statewide residential solar permitting process. Every municipality sets its own rules:

  • Detroit — permits handled through the Buildings, Safety Engineering and Environmental Department (BSEED). Online portal, structural review for any roof-mount, electrical review for the AC side.
  • Dearborn — separate building, electrical, and (for some configurations) mechanical permits. Inspector schedules can be tight; book ahead.
  • Ann Arbor — generally solar-friendly with a streamlined process; specific solar permit checklist available from the city building department.
  • Smaller townships — often have less specific solar guidance, which can cut either way. Sometimes the inspector is reasonable and pragmatic; sometimes they have never seen a solar permit before and the process drags.

Always call your local building department before you buy anything. Ask: do you require sealed engineer drawings for roof-mount, what is your interpretation of the homeowner electrical exemption, do you require a separate battery permit, and how many inspections will the install need. Five minutes on the phone here can save weeks of rework later.

Risks of Pure DIY

DIY solar and battery work is reasonable for a careful, mechanically inclined homeowner — but it is not risk-free, and pretending otherwise does no one any favors. The risks worth taking seriously:

  • Live electrical at the main panel — pulling the main is non-trivial. Even with the main off, the lugs above it are still hot. This is one of the things you are paying the electrician to do correctly.
  • Falls from the roof — statistically the most common solar-install injury, by far. Anchor points, harnesses, and a buddy on the ground are not optional.
  • Lithium battery thermal events — LiFePO4 is the safest commonly available lithium chemistry, but a poorly torqued busbar, an undersized fuse, or a botched BMS connection can still produce a fire. Follow the manufacturer's commissioning checklist exactly.
  • Voided warranties — most inverter manufacturers require commissioning by a certified installer or an explicit homeowner self-commissioning protocol. Skip it and you may be on your own if a $4,000 inverter dies in year three.
  • Insurance complications — some homeowner's insurance policies require disclosure of solar/battery installations, and unpermitted work can create coverage gaps. Notify your insurer in writing.
