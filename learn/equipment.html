Inverters

The inverter is the single most important architectural decision in the system. It dictates whether the system is staged or all-in-one, whether you can upgrade modularly, whether you can run Dynamic ESS for automatic rate optimization, and how DIY-friendly the install is. The two ecosystems worth seriously considering for DTE-area DIY builds are Victron and EG4.

Total Victron system cost is comparable to EG4 once these are added in, but the modular architecture allows staged purchasing — you can buy the inverter and Cerbo now, add the MPPT and panels later when the roof is replaced.

Batteries

Battery cost dropped from $400+/kWh to under $100/kWh over the past five years, and that is the single biggest reason residential storage now pencils out without the tax credit. The dominant chemistry for residential is LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) — safer than NMC, longer cycle life, and lower energy density that does not matter for stationary applications.

Note: 48V systems are recommended for DIY due to safety — terminal voltage is below the typical shock-hazard threshold. 400V high-voltage systems should be professionally installed. The Docan Panda at $86/kWh is the current value leader for residential storage but is a Chinese direct-import brand with less established US warranty support than EG4 or LG. Buy from a US distributor (Houston-area freight is common) and budget time for shipping logistics.

Solar Panels

Panels are the most commoditized part of the system. Module pricing has been remarkably stable around $0.30–0.40/W for the last several years, and the differences between Tier-1 and bulk/sale-priced panels are smaller than most other equipment choices.

Comfort Equipment

Comfort equipment is often the highest-ROI part of the stack on a per-dollar basis — not because the dollar savings are huge but because the upfront cost is low and the lifestyle improvement is real. Cool Currents in particular is a free program that shifts AC load to a flat 17¢ rate; it pays back instantly the moment you enroll.

Recommended Setup

Staged Victron + Docan Panda — The Whole-Site Recommendation

For the homeowner staging investment, the recommended approach is two Victron MultiPlus-II 48/3000 inverters (split phase, 6 kW output), a Cerbo GX for monitoring and Dynamic ESS, and a single Docan Panda 32 kWh battery — total around $5,865 including shipping before any solar.

This Stage 1 build runs battery arbitrage on D1.13 and pays back in 8–12 years on house only, 7–8 years with an EV in the mix. Solar can be added in Stage 2 (when the roof is replaced) by adding a Victron SmartSolar MPPT 250/100 and 8 × 400W Phono panels at $145 — roughly another $2,460 — for a combined ~$8,325 system with $900–1,100/year savings and an 8–9 year combined payback.

Stage 3 (when EV and heat pump both come online) adds a second pair of MultiPlus-II 48/5000 inverters in parallel, additional solar, and optionally a second Docan Panda. Every dollar spent in Stage 1 is still in service in Stage 3 — nothing is thrown away.

  • Modular: add inverters, MPPTs, and batteries as budget allows.
  • Dynamic ESS automates the rate-arbitrage logic. No custom code required.
  • 48V system safe for DIY maintenance, expansion, and battery swap.
  • Compatible with virtually any 48V battery — you are not locked to one brand.
  • Lower upfront cost than EG4 all-in-one when solar is not needed yet.
