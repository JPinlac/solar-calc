The Stacking Strategy

No single decision turns a DTE bill upside down. The trick is that each optimization compounds with the next: a plan switch alone saves a little, but a plan switch plus Cool Currents plus battery arbitrage plus right-sized solar plus overnight EV charging produces savings several times the sum of the parts. This page walks through the five layers in the order they typically make sense to stack, and what each one unlocks for the next.

The Five Layers, In Order

  1. Layer 1 — Cool Currents removes AC from the main meter

    For most Southeast Michigan households, central air conditioning is the single biggest summer load — often 30–45% of June/July/August kWh. DTE's Cool Currents program puts the central AC on a separate meter at an effective flat rate of around 17¢/kWh in summer, with a small caveat: DTE may briefly cycle the unit during high-demand events. For most homes this is invisible.

    Why this is Layer 1: moving the AC off your primary meter shrinks the load profile you are about to optimize against. It also means that when you later switch your main meter to D1.13 — which has a more expensive peak window than D1.11 — your central AC is not the thing getting clobbered by the higher peak rate, because it is no longer on that meter.

  2. Layer 2 — Pick the right TOU plan for what's installed

    DTE's three live residential TOU plans target three different load profiles. The right choice depends on what equipment you have:

    • D1.11 (3pm–7pm peak) — the default, best for households that cannot meaningfully shift any load. Shortest peak window of any plan.
    • D1.2 (11am–7pm peak) — longer peak window but cheaper off-peak. Best for households that have solar covering most of the 11am–7pm window directly.
    • D1.13 Overnight Savers — three-tier with a super off-peak rate of 11.74¢ from 1am–7am. Best for any household with a battery, an EV, or both.

    Notice the pattern: as you install more equipment, you move along the plan ladder. No equipment → D1.11. Solar-only → D1.2. Battery, or EV, or both → D1.13. The "best plan" is not a property of the household, it is a property of the household plus its equipment.

  3. Layer 3 — Battery arbitrage on D1.13

    On D1.13 the spread between super off-peak (11.74¢) and summer peak (35.56¢) is 23.82¢/kWh. A battery that charges between 1am and 7am and discharges during the 3pm–7pm peak captures that full spread on every cycle. A 32 kWh Docan Panda pack with realistic 80% useful depth-of-discharge can shift roughly 25 kWh per day in the months where the spread is widest, which is the engine of the whole arbitrage case.

    Battery arbitrage is profitable without solar. This is the key insight that drives the Stage 1 of the recommended Victron + Docan Panda build — you can start with battery only, capture most of the savings, and add solar later when the roof needs replacement.

  4. Layer 4 — Right-sized solar for self-consumption

    DTE's distributed-generation program pays roughly 7.75¢/kWh for off-peak exports and ~14¢/kWh for summer peak exports — both well below the retail rates you pay to import. The dollar value of solar therefore depends entirely on whether you consume it yourself. A 3.2 kW array (8 × 400W panels) sized to roughly match daily household usage is usually the sweet spot: enough to fill the battery and run the home midday, not so much that you are giving away kWh at export rates.

    A battery is what lets solar capture full retail value. Without storage, midday solar surplus gets exported at 7.75¢ and you buy it back at 19–35¢ in the evening — a guaranteed loss on every exported kWh. With storage, the same kWh charges the battery midday and discharges into peak, capturing the full peak rate instead.

  5. Layer 5 — Mini-split for comfort and supplemental heat

    A 12,000 BTU mini-split installed in a problem room (often a bonus room over the garage, a west-facing living room, or a finished basement) costs $700–1,500 and serves two purposes: it handles the rooms central AC struggles with, and it operates as a heat pump in winter at a COP of 3+, displacing expensive resistance or propane heat in shoulder seasons. Combined with Cool Currents on the central unit, you get better comfort and lower cooling/heating cost.

  6. Layer 6 — EV charging at super off-peak

    An EV charged exclusively at the D1.13 super off-peak rate of 11.74¢/kWh costs roughly 3.4¢ per mile at typical EV efficiency (3.5 mi/kWh). For a 12,000 mile/year driver, that is about $400/year in "fuel" — about a quarter of what a comparable ICE car burns in gasoline. The EV is also what most often pushes a household over the threshold where battery arbitrage really pays, because EV charging stacks neatly on top of the battery charge window in the 1am–7am block.

The Whole-Greater-Than-Sum Principle

Each layer alone saves a little. Stacked together they transform the economics. The plan switch alone saves maybe $50/year. Add Cool Currents and you are at $200. Add battery arbitrage and you clear $500. Add solar and you push past $900. Add EV overnight charging on top and you are looking at $1,400+ annually — and that is before you factor in any rate inflation over the system's life.

Why the Order Matters

You can stack these in different orders, but the suggested sequence has a financial logic. Cool Currents is free and saves money immediately, so it goes first. The TOU plan switch is also free and locks in the rate structure all later equipment will exploit, so it goes second. Battery arbitrage is the highest-return capital expense per dollar — pays back fastest, works without solar, and is the foundation everything else builds on. Solar is added once the battery exists to capture its value at retail rates. The mini-split is comfort-driven and can slot in any time. EV overnight charging is often the trigger that takes the whole stack from "nice savings" to "transformative savings," because it doubles or triples nighttime usage at the cheapest rate of the day.

The other reason the order matters: each layer's payback calculation depends on having the previous layers in place. Battery arbitrage savings are computed against D1.13 rates, which only exist if you have made the plan switch. Solar self-consumption value is computed against retail rates, which requires the battery to capture the late-evening rates. Trying to evaluate any single layer in isolation undersells what the stack actually does.
